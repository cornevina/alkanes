1. Introduction to Alkanes

Alkanes are the simplest family of organic compounds, known as saturated hydrocarbons. This means they consist only of carbon (C) and hydrogen (H) atoms and contain only single covalent bonds.

Key Characteristics:

  • General Formula: CnH2n+2 (for non-cyclic alkanes). For cycloalkanes, it's CnH2n.
  • Bonding: All carbon atoms are sp3 hybridized, resulting in a tetrahedral geometry with bond angles of 109.5°.
  • Reactivity: Generally unreactive due to the strength and non-polar nature of their C-C and C-H bonds.

Nomenclature (IUPAC Naming)

Naming alkanes follows a systematic set of rules:

  1. Find the Parent Chain: Identify the longest continuous chain of carbon atoms.
  2. Number the Chain: Start numbering from the end that gives the substituents (branches) the lowest possible numbers.
  3. Name the Substituents: Name the alkyl groups attached to the parent chain (e.g., -CH3 is methyl, -CH2CH3 is ethyl).
  4. Assemble the Name: List substituents alphabetically, preceded by their position number. Use prefixes (di-, tri-, tetra-) for multiple identical substituents.
Example: Name the following compound:

Answer: 2,2,4-trimethylpentane

Explanation: The longest chain is 5 carbons (pentane). Numbering from the right gives the methyl groups the lowest numbers (2, 2, and 4). Since there are three methyl groups, we use "trimethyl".

2. Isomerism in Alkanes

Isomers are compounds with the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements. Alkanes with four or more carbons exhibit constitutional (or structural) isomerism.

Constitutional Isomers

These isomers have the same molecular formula but different connectivity of atoms (different structural formulae). This leads to different physical properties.

Example: C4H10

Butane

CH₃-CH₂-CH₂-CH₃

(b.p. -0.5 °C)

2-methylpropane (isobutane)

CH₃-CH(CH₃)-CH₃

(b.p. -11.7 °C)

Note: As the number of carbons increases, the number of possible isomers grows rapidly. Pentane (C₅H₁₂) has 3 isomers, while decane (C₁₀H₂₂) has 75!

3. Physical Properties

Boiling Points

Boiling points increase with the number of carbon atoms. This is due to stronger instantaneous dipole-induced dipole (van der Waals) forces between larger molecules with more electrons.

Branching lowers the boiling point. Branched molecules are more compact, reducing the surface area for intermolecular contact, thus weakening the van der Waals forces.

Solubility

Alkanes are non-polar. Following the "like dissolves like" principle, they are:

  • Insoluble in polar solvents like water.
  • Soluble in non-polar organic solvents (e.g., hexane, benzene).

4. Chemical Reactions

General Unreactivity

Alkanes are inert to most common reagents like acids, bases, and oxidizing agents. This is because their C-C and C-H sigma bonds are very strong and non-polar, offering no sites for attack by polar reagents.

However, they undergo a few important reactions under specific, energetic conditions.

Combustion

This is a highly exothermic reaction, which is why alkanes are excellent fuels.

Complete Combustion: Occurs with an excess of oxygen, producing carbon dioxide and water.

CnH2n+2 + ( (3n+1)/2 ) O2 → nCO2 + (n+1)H2O

Incomplete Combustion: Occurs in a limited supply of oxygen. It produces toxic carbon monoxide (CO) and/or carbon (soot) along with water.

Free Radical Substitution (Halogenation)

Alkanes react with halogens (typically Cl2 or Br2) in the presence of UV light. This is a chain reaction where hydrogen atoms are successively replaced by halogen atoms. The reaction is hard to control and often yields a mixture of mono- and poly-substituted products.

General Reaction: R-H + X2 --(UV light)--> R-X + H-X

Cracking

Cracking is the process of breaking down large, less useful alkane molecules into smaller, more valuable ones (like petrol components and alkenes) by heating them to high temperatures.

Thermal Cracking: High temperature (450-750°C) and pressure. Produces a high percentage of alkenes, which are feedstocks for polymers.

e.g., C12H26 → C8H18 + 2C2H4

Catalytic Cracking: Lower temperature (around 600°C) with a catalyst (e.g., zeolites like Al2O3/SiO2). Produces branched alkanes and aromatic hydrocarbons, which are components of high-octane petrol.

5. Free Radical Substitution In-Depth

This reaction proceeds via a chain mechanism involving highly reactive intermediates called free radicals (species with an unpaired electron). Let's explore the chlorination of ethane (CH3CH3).

Step 1: Initiation

UV light provides the energy to break the Cl-Cl bond via homolytic fission, creating two chlorine free radicals.

Cl-Cl --UV Light--> 2 Cl•

Each atom takes one electron from the shared pair.

Step 2: Propagation

These are chain-carrying steps. A radical reacts to form a new molecule and another radical.

(a) CH3CH3 + Cl• → •CH2CH3 + HCl
(b) •CH2CH3 + Cl2 → CH3CH2Cl + Cl•

The chlorine radical regenerated in step (b) can start the cycle again.

Step 3: Termination

The reaction stops when two radicals collide and combine, removing radicals from the system.

Cl• + Cl• → Cl2
•CH2CH3 + Cl• → CH3CH2Cl
•CH2CH3 + •CH2CH3 → CH3CH2CH2CH3 (butane byproduct)

Selectivity in Halogenation: Radical Stability

Not all hydrogen atoms are equally likely to be substituted. The stability of the intermediate free radical determines the major product.

Stability Order: Tertiary (3°) > Secondary (2°) > Primary (1°) > Methyl.

This is because alkyl groups are electron-donating, which helps to stabilize the electron-deficient carbon radical.

Example (Propane): When propane (CH3CH2CH3) is chlorinated, 2-chloropropane (from a more stable secondary radical) is the major product over 1-chloropropane (from a less stable primary radical), even though there are more primary hydrogens.

6. Environmental Impact & Sources

The use of alkanes has significant environmental consequences.

Air Pollution

  • Carbon Monoxide (CO): Toxic gas from incomplete combustion.
  • Oxides of Nitrogen (NOx): From high temperatures in engines, causes smog and acid rain.
  • Unburnt Hydrocarbons: Contribute to photochemical smog.

Catalytic Converters

Cars use catalytic converters with Pt and Rh catalysts to convert pollutants into harmless gases.

2CO + 2NO → 2CO2 + N2

Enhanced Greenhouse Effect

Carbon Dioxide (CO2), the main product of complete combustion, is a major greenhouse gas. Its accumulation in the atmosphere traps heat, leading to global warming and climate change.

Petroleum as a Finite Resource

Alkanes are primarily obtained from crude oil (petroleum) through fractional distillation. As a fossil fuel, petroleum is a finite, non-renewable resource. This highlights the importance of energy conservation and recycling.
